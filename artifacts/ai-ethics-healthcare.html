AI Bias in Healthcare:
When Algorithms Inherit Inequality

An interactive case study analyzing racial bias in a healthcare algorithm that affected 200 million patients — and what it teaches us about building ethical AI systems.

The Incident

Case Study Overview

In 2019, researchers at UC Berkeley and Harvard published findings in Science revealing that a widely used healthcare algorithm, developed by Optum, exhibited significant racial bias. The algorithm used healthcare spending as a proxy for health need, but because Black patients historically had less access to care (and therefore lower spending), the system systematically underestimated their illness severity.

Correcting this bias would have increased the proportion of Black patients flagged for additional care from 17.7% to 46.5%.

The Mechanism

How the Bias Entered the System

The algorithm's designers chose a seemingly rational target variable: healthcare spending. Higher spending should correlate with greater health need, right? But this assumption collapses when access to care is itself unequal.

Critical Analysis

Three Ethical Issues

Click each issue to explore why it matters, the analysis of its implications, and a proposed solution.

Ethical Issue 1: Using Cost as a Proxy for Health Need

Why This Matters

This is the foundational flaw. The designers chose healthcare spending as the target variable, assuming higher spending correlates with greater health need. This assumption fails to account for systemic inequities in healthcare access that result in lower spending for Black patients even when they are sicker. It is a textbook example of how a seemingly neutral technical decision can encode structural racism.

Analysis of Implications

The choice of proxy variable meant the algorithm was not predicting illness — it was predicting cost, which is shaped by insurance coverage, geographic access to providers, historical discrimination in healthcare delivery, and individual ability to navigate the system.

Because these factors disproportionately disadvantage Black patients, the algorithm effectively learned that Black patients are "healthier" than they actually are. This resulted in fewer Black patients being referred to care management programs, creating a feedback loop where reduced access led to reduced spending, which led to lower risk scores, which led to even less care.

According to Obermeyer et al. (2019), Black patients at the 97th percentile risk score had 26% more chronic conditions than white patients at the same score, yet received equivalent or less follow-up care.

Proposed Solution

Algorithm designers should use direct health outcome measures — number of chronic conditions, lab results, clinical severity indicators — rather than spending as the prediction target. When proxy variables are necessary, they should be validated against ground-truth health data across demographic groups before deployment, with mandatory disparate impact testing. The original researchers demonstrated that switching to a variable combining cost and health predictions reduced bias by 86% (Obermeyer et al., 2019).

Ethical Issue 2: Lack of Demographic Auditing Before Deployment

Why This Matters

The algorithm was deployed across major hospital systems affecting millions of patients without any published audit of its performance across racial groups. This reflects a broader industry pattern where models are evaluated on aggregate accuracy metrics without examining whether that accuracy is equitable across subpopulations.

Analysis of Implications

As Vyas et al. (2020) documented in their review of race correction in clinical algorithms, many widely used medical tools adjust their outputs based on race in ways that systematically direct fewer resources to minority patients.

Aggregate metrics can mask dramatic inequities: a model that is 90% accurate overall might be 95% accurate for white patients and 70% accurate for Black patients. Without mandatory subgroup analysis, these gaps remain invisible until the harm is already done.

Proposed Solution

Healthcare organizations deploying AI systems should require pre-deployment fairness audits that evaluate model performance stratified by race, ethnicity, gender, and socioeconomic status. These audits should be conducted by independent third parties and their results should be publicly accessible, following the model of FDA drug approval transparency.

Ethical Issue 3: Amplification of Systemic Inequity Through Scale

Why This Matters

What makes this incident particularly concerning is not just that bias existed, but that it was operationalized at massive scale. The Optum algorithm was used to guide care decisions for approximately 200 million patients annually. When bias is embedded in a system that operates at that scale, the harm is not incremental — it is systemic.

Analysis of Implications

Before algorithmic decision-making, biased care decisions were made by individual practitioners in individual settings — harmful, but localized. When the same biased logic is encoded in a centralized algorithm and deployed across thousands of hospitals, it standardizes and scales the inequity.

As the ACLU (2021) noted, algorithms in healthcare may only worsen medical racism when deployed without adequate oversight. The combination of algorithmic bias and systemic deployment created conditions where structural racism operated at machine speed.

Proposed Solution

Organizations deploying AI at scale should implement continuous monitoring systems that track outcomes by demographic group in real time, not just at the point of initial validation. Regulatory frameworks should require impact assessments proportional to the scale of deployment — a tool affecting 200 million patients should face substantially more rigorous scrutiny than a pilot program.

Compounding Crisis

COVID-19: When Bias Meets a Pandemic

The COVID-19 pandemic created a stress test for healthcare AI systems. The same algorithmic biases that were identified in the Optum study took on new urgency when AI-driven tools were deployed for triage, resource allocation, and vaccine prioritization during a crisis that already disproportionately affected communities of color.

The Amplification Effect

During COVID-19, hospitals increasingly relied on algorithmic tools for triage, resource allocation, and care prioritization. If these tools inherit the same blind spots — evaluating aggregate performance without demographic stratification — they risk allocating ventilators, ICU beds, and vaccines in ways that deepen existing disparities.

AI-driven triage systems did not just reflect past bias — they actively shaped who received timely treatment during a crisis where timing was the difference between life and death.

The combination of algorithmic bias and pandemic urgency created conditions where structural racism operated at machine speed, with consequences measured not in risk scores but in lives.

Scenario 1: Aggregate vs. Equitable Accuracy

A hospital deploys an AI risk prediction model. The vendor reports 92% overall accuracy on their validation dataset. The hospital's leadership is satisfied and approves system-wide rollout. A junior data analyst asks whether accuracy was tested across demographic groups. The response: "92% is 92%. The model works."

What is the most important next step?

A. Accept the 92% figure — it exceeds the industry benchmark.
B. Require a demographic breakdown of accuracy before rollout.
C. Remove race data from the model to prevent discrimination.
D. Add a disclaimer that the model may not be equally accurate for all groups.

Scenario 2: The Proxy Variable Trap

Your team builds a model to predict which patients need follow-up care. You use number of past doctor visits as a key input feature. A colleague points out that patients in rural areas visit doctors less frequently — not because they are healthier, but because the nearest clinic is 90 minutes away.

What does this scenario illustrate?

A. A data quality issue — rural data should be excluded.
B. An overfitting problem — the model is too sensitive to visit frequency.
C. A proxy variable problem — visit frequency reflects access, not health need.
D. A labeling error — visit frequency should be a target, not an input.
Critical Thinking

How My Thinking Evolved

The Assumption That Changed

When I began this analysis, my assumption was that AI bias in healthcare was primarily a data quality problem — that if you cleaned the data and removed obvious demographic variables, the algorithm would be fair. By the end, I understood that the bias was architectural. It was embedded in the choice of what to predict, not just the data used for prediction. A model can be technically accurate and statistically sound while still being deeply unjust if the target variable itself encodes inequality.

How This Shapes My Future Approach

This analysis directly influences my approach to AI development in supply chain software. The predictive models I build are used for labor scheduling, demand forecasting, and resource allocation — all decisions that directly affect workers' livelihoods and communities' access to goods.

I will now question not just the accuracy of a model, but the equity of its target variable. Who benefits from the way we have defined "success" in our optimization? Whose needs are invisible in the data we have chosen to collect?

These are the questions that separate a technically competent AI practitioner from an ethical AI leader.
