The Evolution of Artificial Intelligence & Machine Learning: An Interactive Timeline

Workshop 1 · Discussion 1.3 · Historical Research

Introduction

This artifact is an interactive, visually rich timeline that traces the evolution of AI and machine learning from Alan Turing's foundational 1950 paper through the current era of large language models and multimodal reasoning systems.

Description

The timeline covers 20 key milestones organized into color-coded eras: Early AI (1950s–1970s), AI Winters, Revival periods, the Deep Learning breakthrough era (2011–2017), and the LLM era (2020–present). Each entry includes the year, event name, detailed description, and key figures or organizations involved. The timeline features scroll-triggered animations, responsive design, and an alternating left-right layout for visual engagement.

Objective

To research and present the historical development of AI/ML in a format that is both educational and visually compelling, demonstrating the ability to synthesize complex technical history into an accessible resource.

Process

I studied three source types recommended by the course: a narrative video documentary on AI history, an IEEE scholarly article on AI evolution, and interactive data visualizations from Our World in Data. I then designed and coded a custom HTML/CSS/JavaScript timeline with era-based color coding, hover interactions, and intersection observer animations for a dynamic scroll experience.

Tools & Technologies Used

HTML5, CSS3 (gradients, animations, media queries), JavaScript (Intersection Observer API), Claude AI (research assistance), Google Fonts.

Value Proposition

AI Lab: Generative AI Exploration & Freight Planner Chatbot

Workshop 1 · Assignment 1.4 · Hands-On AI Lab

Introduction

This artifact documents my hands-on exploration of three generative AI tools and culminates in a functional chatbot prototype designed to solve a real pain point in retail freight management.

Description

The lab had two parts. First, I tested and documented three AI tools: Claude (LLM testing with supply chain prompts), a Custom GPT Business Strategy Advisor (iterative prompting for AI implementation), and STORM AI (multi-perspective research synthesis). Second, using design thinking methodology, I identified a problem faced by store leaders—needing fast, conversational access to freight status without navigating multiple systems—and built a functional Freight Planner Chatbot prototype in HTML and JavaScript.

Objective

To demonstrate proficiency in using generative AI tools, analyze their outputs critically, and apply design thinking to develop a practical AI-based solution to a real workplace problem.

Process

For the AI tool exploration, I tested each tool with supply-chain-specific prompts, varying the input to observe how responses changed with added context, personal insight, and pushback. For the chatbot, I followed a design thinking process: empathize (identify store leaders' frustration with fragmented freight data), define (the need for a conversational interface), ideate (sketch chatbot features), prototype (build a working HTML/JS chatbot with keyword-based response matching, quick-action sidebar, and realistic freight data), and test (verify responses across freight, stocking, delivery, team assignment, and inventory scenarios).

Tools & Technologies Used

Claude AI, Custom GPT (OpenAI), STORM AI, HTML5, CSS3, JavaScript, Design Thinking methodology.

Value Proposition

Unique Value: This artifact goes beyond theoretical understanding. It shows that I can evaluate AI tools critically, identify real operational problems, and build working prototypes. The chatbot directly addresses a daily challenge I face at Walmart, demonstrating practical AI application in supply chain.

Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning: A Retail Supply Chain Analysis

Workshop 2 · Assignment 2.3 · Technical Report

Introduction

This technical report analyzes the practical differences between machine learning and deep learning through two real-world applications in retail supply chain, demonstrating not just knowledge of the algorithms but an understanding of when and why each approach is the right fit.

Description

The report examines two case studies. First, demand forecasting using traditional ML: Walmart uses algorithms like XGBoost and random forests with structured tabular data (historical sales, seasonality, promotions, weather, store attributes) to predict product demand. ML is ideal because the data is structured, relationships are interpretable, training is fast, and results are explainable to supply chain managers. Second, shelf monitoring using deep learning: retailers deploy camera-based systems using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to detect out-of-stock items and misplaced products in real time. Deep learning excels because it processes raw image data, automatically learns hierarchical visual features, and adapts to varying lighting and camera angles. The report includes a detailed comparison table contrasting data type, feature engineering, algorithms, computational cost, interpretability, data volume, and best-fit applications.

Objective

To differentiate between machine learning and deep learning by selecting and analyzing real-world applications relevant to retail supply chain, and to explain why each approach is suitable (and the other is not) for the given problem.

Process

I began by studying the course lesson materials on ML and DL fundamentals. I then researched real-world applications at Walmart and other retailers, selecting demand forecasting and shelf monitoring because they are directly relevant to my work and clearly illustrate the strengths and limitations of each approach. I drafted the analysis for each example, explaining both why the chosen approach is suitable and why the alternative is not. Finally, I created a comparison table and revised the language to be accessible to both technical and non-technical readers.

Tools & Technologies Used

Microsoft Word, course lesson materials on ML and DL, industry research on retail AI applications.

Value Proposition

Unique Value: This report demonstrates the ability to make informed technology decisions, not just understand the theory. By grounding the analysis in actual retail operations, it shows practical judgment about which AI tools to deploy for which problems, a critical skill for anyone leading AI adoption.

Relevance: Retail and supply chain professionals increasingly need to evaluate AI solutions. This artifact provides a model for how to think about ML vs. DL trade-offs in context, making it a useful reference for colleagues, managers, or hiring teams evaluating AI literacy.
